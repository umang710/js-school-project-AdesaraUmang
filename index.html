Timeline of Computer Gaming

1958: Tennis for Two

Physicist William Higinbotham develops "Tennis for Two" using an oscilloscope, widely considered the first interactive analog computer game.

1962: Spacewar!

MIT student Steve Russell creates "Spacewar!" on a PDP-1, one of the first digital computer games and a precursor to multiplayer space combat games.

1972: Pong

Atari releases "Pong," the first commercially successful arcade video game, launching the video game industry as a mainstream entertainment sector.

1977: Atari 2600

The Atari 2600 introduces cartridge-based gaming, setting the standard for home console design and distribution.

1980: Pac-Man

Namco's "Pac-Man" becomes a global arcade sensation, introducing character-based gaming and attracting a broader demographic, including women.

1981: IBM PC Launch

The IBM Personal Computer is released, popularizing DOS-based PC gaming and opening the door for future titles like King's Quest and Wolfenstein.

1983: Video Game Crash

The North American video game market crashes due to oversaturation, lack of quality control, and competition from home computers.

1985: Nintendo Entertainment System (NES)

Nintendo releases the NES in North America, revitalizing the industry and establishing franchises like Super Mario, The Legend of Zelda, and Metroid.

1989: Game Boy

Nintendo launches the Game Boy with "Tetris," creating the first successful handheld gaming platform and laying the foundation for mobile gaming.

1991: Sonic the Hedgehog

Sega introduces Sonic as a rival mascot to Mario, boosting sales of the Sega Genesis and establishing Sega as a major console player.

1993: Doom

id Software's "Doom" revolutionizes first-person shooters with 3D graphics, modding culture, and network multiplayer gameplay.

1996: Nintendo 64

The N64 brings 3D console gaming to the mainstream with titles like "Super Mario 64" and "The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time."

1998: StarCraft

Blizzard releases "StarCraft," a real-time strategy game that becomes a global esport, especially in South Korea.

2001: Halo: Combat Evolved

"Halo" debuts as the flagship title for the original Xbox, revolutionizing console FPS mechanics and multiplayer gameplay.

2004: World of Warcraft

Blizzard's "World of Warcraft" launches, setting new standards in MMORPGs with its expansive world and subscription-based model.

2007: iPhone and Mobile Gaming Era

The iPhone launches, followed by the App Store in 2008, transforming mobile phones into a dominant gaming platform.

2011: Minecraft

Mojang releases "Minecraft," a sandbox game emphasizing creativity and survival, later acquired by Microsoft in 2014.

2013: PlayStation 4 and Xbox One

The PS4 and Xbox One launch, bringing high-definition gaming, digital downloads, and advanced online features to consoles.

2016: Pokémon GO

Niantic’s "Pokémon GO" merges augmented reality and geolocation, creating a global cultural event in mobile gaming.

2020: PS5 and Xbox Series X

Next-gen consoles launch, emphasizing ultra-fast SSDs, ray tracing, and 4K gaming performance.

2023–2025: Rise of AI and Cloud Gaming

AI-assisted NPCs, procedural generation, and services like Xbox Cloud Gaming and NVIDIA GeForce NOW signal the future of streaming and intelligent gaming experiences.
